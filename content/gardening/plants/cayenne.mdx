Cayenne is my go-to for drying and grinding into chilli powder. Plants are extremely productive and the long, slender pods dry beautifully on the vine or strung up indoors.

## Varieties I Grow

### Long Thin Cayenne
The classic variety. Slim pods 10–15 cm long, turning brilliant red at maturity. Heat level around 30,000–50,000 SHU.

### Ring of Fire
A slightly hotter selection bred for early ripening — great for shorter growing seasons.

## Growing Tips

1. **Thin-Walled Fruit Dries Faster**: Cayenne's thin walls make it far better for drying than thicker varieties
2. **Leave on the Plant**: Allow fruit to turn fully red before harvesting for drying purposes
3. **String Drying**: Tie harvested red pods together and hang in a warm, airy spot indoors for 3–4 weeks
4. **Ristras**: Braiding pods into a decorative ristra looks great and dries them effectively
5. **Gloves When Processing**: Capsaicin from cayenne will linger on hands for hours — always wear gloves

## Common Problems

- **Slow Ripening**: Cayenne needs a long season; start seeds as early as possible
- **Spider Mites**: Encouraged by hot dry conditions; increase humidity and use neem oil spray
- **Hollow Pods**: Usually caused by inadequate pollination during a heat wave

## Harvest

For fresh use, pick when red and firm. For drying, let pods start to wrinkle on the plant, which concentrates the flavour and reduces drying time. A single productive cayenne plant can yield 50–100 pods in a good season.
